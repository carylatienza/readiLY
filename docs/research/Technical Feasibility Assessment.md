# **Technical Feasibility Assessment: Offline-First Automatic Speech Recognition for Oral Reading Fluency in Philippine Languages**

The development of an offline-first, mobile-based Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) application to assess the Oral Reading Fluency (ORF) of public school students in the Philippines represents a highly complex intersection of edge computing, low-resource linguistic modeling, and pediatric acoustic analysis. Specifically targeting Grades 1–6 learners reading in Filipino (Tagalog), regional languages (Cebuano, Ilocano, Hiligaynon, Bikol, Waray), and Philippine-accented English introduces significant acoustic and algorithmic challenges. The system must operate without internet connectivity on budget-constrained Android tablets provided under the Department of Education (DepEd) Computerization Program, mandating extreme optimization of neural network architectures. Furthermore, the goal is not merely to transcribe open-domain speech, but to execute granular miscue detection—identifying phoneme-level decoding, omissions, insertions, and substitutions against a known reference text.  
This assessment exhaustively evaluates the feasibility of constructing such a system. The analysis catalogs existing speech corpora, benchmarks state-of-the-art open-source acoustic models, examines the severe degradation of ASR systems when exposed to child speech, details the specific hardware constraints of on-device inference engines, outlines self-supervised fine-tuning pathways, and explores the algorithmic necessity of constrained decoding for automated fluency scoring.

## **1\. Catalog of Speech Datasets for Philippine Languages**

The foundational requirement for any ASR system is the availability of high-quality, phonetically balanced, and demographically representative audio corpora. Historically, the languages of the Philippines, despite comprising tens of millions of native speakers, have been categorized as low-resource within the natural language processing (NLP) domain. However, the landscape has evolved significantly over the past half-decade, driven by academic consortia and open-source initiatives. The corpus ecosystem is divided into generalized adult datasets, which are essential for pre-training foundational acoustic models, and specialized child-speech datasets, which are critical for fine-tuning architectures to pediatric vocal tract characteristics and early-reader disfluencies.

### **1.1 Generalized and Adult Speech Corpora**

While adult speech data cannot be utilized directly to train an accurate pediatric reading tracker, it provides the massive volume required for self-supervised learning (SSL) algorithms to map the fundamental phonotactics and sub-word units of Philippine languages.  
The most extensive and meticulously curated dataset currently available is the Philippine Languages Database (UP-DSP-PLD), developed by the Digital Signal Processing Laboratory at the University of the Philippines Diliman. The PLD contains over 454 hours of recorded speech encompassing ten distinct languages: Tagalog/Filipino, Cebuano, Kapampangan, Hiligaynon, Ilocano, Bikolano, Waray, Tausug, Pangasinan, and English. The corpus was constructed using a rigorous methodology wherein a custom recording tool presented random subsets of read-speech prompts and spontaneous conversational questions to over 1,000 speakers. Crucially for downstream normalization, the PLD captures exhaustive demographic metadata at the point of recording, including the speaker's age, gender, profession, and the first language of both the speaker and their parents, allowing researchers to isolate specific regional dialects and accents. The dataset is distributed under a non-commercial research license and is accessible either through direct institutional request to the UP-DSP laboratory or via the Mozilla Data Collective.  
The Mozilla Common Voice (MCV) project represents the largest crowdsourced, public-domain (CC0 license) speech repository globally. However, its coverage of Philippine languages remains highly skewed. As of the MCV v17 and subsequent releases, Tagalog features an active localization and validation pipeline, yet the volume of fully validated, high-quality hours remains a fraction of what is available for high-resource languages. Furthermore, the demographic distribution within MCV is entirely dependent on volunteer contributors, meaning the presence of child speech is purely incidental and statistically negligible. Additionally, analyses of MCV datasets reveal that a significant percentage of crowd-sourced audio files suffer from truncation, background noise, or extreme brevity (utterances under four seconds), which limits their utility for training robust continuous-speech acoustic models.  
For highly structured read-speech, SIL International’s Bloom Speech corpus offers a highly relevant alternative. Developed alongside the Bloom Library software—which equips non-dominant language communities to create localized literature—the dataset contains 428 hours of audio across 56 global languages. Within the Philippine context, it includes 352 training cuts for Tagalog and 553 training cuts for Cebuano. Because the audio consists of individuals reading educational and literary texts aloud, the cadence and acoustic environment closely mirror the target use-case of an oral reading fluency application. The dataset is accessible via HuggingFace under the SIL RAIL-M license, which permits non-commercial use while explicitly prohibiting applications that generate harmful outputs or discriminate against indigenous populations.  
Other repositories, such as OpenSLR, primarily host test suites or highly specific corpora (e.g., SLR84 for handwriting, SLR81 for Burmese) and currently lack dedicated, large-scale Philippine language audio sets. The FLEURS (Few-shot Learning Evaluation of Universal Representations of Speech) benchmark, built upon the FLoRes-101 translation dataset, provides approximately 12 hours of n-way parallel speech for 102 languages, including Tagalog and Cebuano. While FLEURS is indispensable for evaluating zero-shot cross-lingual transfer, its limited volume precludes its use for from-scratch acoustic training. Finally, the IARPA Babel program provides 213 hours of spontaneous, conversational Tagalog. However, this data consists of 8kHz narrowband telephone speech, which is acoustically incompatible with the wideband audio captured by modern tablet microphones, and it requires a paid commercial or academic license through the Linguistic Data Consortium.

### **1.2 The Existence of Dedicated Child Speech Corpora**

The user query explicitly requests confirmation of whether any Philippine-language child-speech corpus exists anywhere in the world. The definitive answer is yes. While pediatric corpora are notoriously difficult to compile due to ethical, legal, and logistical constraints surrounding minor consent, several academic initiatives have successfully generated dedicated datasets. These corpora are generally not hosted on open, unauthenticated platforms like GitHub or HuggingFace, but are maintained within academic institutional repositories.  
The most significant asset for regional language development is the Bangsamoro K-3 Assessment Tools (BK3AT) Children's Speech Corpus. This monumental dataset was explicitly engineered to support the creation of automated literacy assessment technologies for facilitators and teachers in Kindergarten through Grade 3 education. Funded partially by international educational pathways, the corpus contains 122 hours of exclusive child speech data spanning ten languages: Filipino, English, Bahasa Sug, Chavacano, Iranun, Maguindanaon, Meranaw, Sinama, Teduray, and Yakan. The audio was captured from Grade 1 to Grade 3 students reading prompts of escalating difficulty—progressing from isolated syllables and words to phrases and full passages—ensuring comprehensive coverage of all 60 phonemes present in the target languages. To acquire this dataset, developers must establish a formal research agreement with the contributing institutions, such as De La Salle University or the Ministry of Basic, Higher, and Technical Education (MBHTE) of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao.  
For the national lingua franca, the Children's Filipino Speech Corpus (CFSC) is the premier resource for reading miscue modeling. Developed collaboratively by researchers at UP Diliman and DLSU, the CFSC was designed for the precise application of evaluating automated reading tutors. The dataset comprises approximately eight hours of continuous read speech recorded from 57 students aged 6 to 11 (Grades 2 through 5). What makes the CFSC uniquely valuable is its structural division: approximately five hours consist of fluent, correct pronunciations, while three hours are specifically isolated recordings of actual reading miscues and disfluencies. The creators observed that early Filipino readers exhibit a predictable "speech repair" structure—consisting of a reparandum, an editing term (such as a hesitation pause or repetition), and an alteration—which allows for precise mathematical modeling of the miscue. The corpus includes semi-automated, time-aligned phoneme-level transcriptions, making it the gold standard for training forced-alignment tracking algorithms. Access requires direct engagement with the UP Digital Signal Processing Laboratory.  
Additionally, the Tanglaw Corpus has been utilized in recent Wav2Vec 2.0 adaptation studies focusing on Filipino children's speech, demonstrating that models pre-trained on adult Filipino speech and fine-tuned on child audio yield substantial reductions in Word Error Rate. For conversational linguistics, the Max Planck Institute hosts a 20-hour corpus of caregiver-child interactions featuring two- to four-year-old Tagalog-speaking children in Metro Manila. While this MPI dataset provides rich data for studying early language acquisition, thematic role assignment, and symmetrical voice systems, its highly spontaneous nature makes it less suitable for training the acoustic bounds of read-aloud fluency applications.

## **2\. Baseline ASR Performance and the Child Speech Degradation Paradigm**

To assess the feasibility of deploying offline ASR, it is critical to evaluate how existing, unmodified foundational models perform on Philippine languages, and subsequently, how those same models degrade when confronted with the acoustics of pediatric speech.

### **2.1 Benchmarking Foundational Models on Philippine Languages**

The proliferation of massively multilingual transformer models has dramatically lowered the barrier to entry for transcribing under-resourced languages. Performance is typically measured using the Word Error Rate (WER), where a lower percentage indicates higher accuracy. Benchmarks are predominantly established using the FLEURS dataset.  
OpenAI’s Whisper architecture, a robust sequence-to-sequence (Seq2Seq) encoder-decoder trained on 680,000 hours of weakly supervised audio, demonstrates strong general-purpose capabilities. On the FLEURS Tagalog benchmark, Whisper Large v3 achieves a WER ranging between 12.3% and 18.4%, depending on the specific evaluation framework and tokenization parameters used. Smaller, fine-tuned variants, such as community-trained Whisper Small models, report self-evaluated WERs of approximately 16.6% on Tagalog subsets. However, Whisper's performance relies heavily on the volume of language-specific data in its proprietary training mix. When tested in a zero-shot cross-lingual transfer setting on languages with minimal training representation, such as Cebuano, unmodified Whisper models experience catastrophic accuracy drops, frequently exhibiting WERs exceeding 40%.  
Self-supervised models, particularly Meta’s Massively Multilingual Speech (MMS) framework built on the wav2vec 2.0 and XLS-R architectures, approach the problem differently. These models learn latent phonetic representations from vast quantities of unlabeled audio before undergoing a light fine-tuning phase with transcribed text. An XLS-R 300M model fine-tuned specifically on the Bloom Speech Tagalog dataset achieved a WER of 24.57% and a Character Error Rate (CER) of 7.69%. While the WER appears elevated compared to commercial models, the remarkably low CER indicates that the model is accurately capturing the phonetic components of the language but struggling to segment them into grammatically correct words—a phenomenon highly relevant to reading miscue detection. For comparative context, leading commercial APIs utilizing massive compute clusters (e.g., ElevenLabs Scribe v1, Speechmatics Enhanced) report Tagalog WERs between 7.0% and 12.2%.  
Evaluating Philippine-accented English presents a unique challenge. While specific benchmarks for L2 Philippine English are scarce in global leaderboards, research on accented English highlights a universal "context-utilization gap." ASR systems trained primarily on North American or British dialects suffer severe performance drops when processing L1-transferred phonological traits—such as the substitution of /p/ for /f/ or /b/ for /v/ commonly found in various Philippine linguistic groups. Models like XLS-R have shown strong resilience against accented speech due to their exposure to extreme global phonetic variance during the unsupervised pre-training phase.

### **2.2 The Physics and Linguistics of Child Speech Degradation**

A fundamental risk in developing educational technology is the assumption that an ASR system benchmarked at a 10% WER on adults will maintain that accuracy on children. The research literature demonstrates conclusively that this is false. When modern ASR systems—trained predominantly on adult speech—are applied to children, the relative word error rate increases dramatically, sometimes surging by 40% to 45%, or yielding WERs up to five times higher than adult baselines.  
This degradation is rooted in two distinct categories of variance: acoustic-physiological differences and linguistic-behavioral differences.  
**Acoustic and Physiological Challenges:** Children's vocal tracts are not simply miniature versions of adult anatomy; they possess entirely different acoustic resonant properties. Due to their significantly shorter vocal tracts and smaller vocal folds, the fundamental frequencies (F0) and formants of children's speech are pushed into much higher spectral ranges. A female adult's fundamental frequency averages around 200 Hz, whereas a child's F0 frequently ranges between 250 Hz and 400 Hz. Consequently, the high-frequency acoustic signatures of child speech fall outside the standard filter-bank distributions that an adult-trained neural network has learned to optimize.  
Furthermore, children lack mature motor control over their articulators. This results in extreme intra-speaker and inter-speaker variability. A seven-year-old child may pronounce the same phoneme with vastly different pitch, intensity, and duration across two consecutive sentences. To counteract this, researchers must frequently employ advanced data augmentation techniques, such as Vocal Tract Length Perturbation (VTLP) and formant spectral warping. VTLP mathematically scales the frequency axis of the adult training audio to simulate a shorter vocal tract, artificially expanding the training data and bridging the acoustic mismatch.  
**Linguistic and Behavioral Challenges in Early Readers:** Beyond the pure physics of sound, evaluating Oral Reading Fluency requires analyzing the *process* of acquiring literacy. Beginning readers in Grades 1 through 3 do not speak in continuous, fluid sentences. Their speech is characterized by:

1. **Phoneme-Level Decoding:** Children will frequently "sound out" words before committing to them, producing elongated, isolated phonemes (e.g., uttering "/b/ ... /a/ ... /t/ ... bat").  
2. **Syllable-by-Syllable Cadence:** Tagalog and many regional Philippine languages possess highly transparent, syllabic orthographies. As a result, early readers adopt a rigid, staccato, syllable-by-syllable reading rhythm that destroys the coarticulation cues ASR models rely upon to predict word boundaries.  
3. **High Disfluency Densities:** Read-aloud sessions are riddled with hesitation pauses, false starts, heavy breathing into the microphone, and repetition.  
4. **Orthographic Guessing:** When confronting an unknown word, struggling readers often pronounce the first syllable correctly and invent a phonotactically plausible suffix, resulting in out-of-vocabulary (OOV) non-words.

Standard end-to-end ASR models fail profoundly at handling these behaviors because they are optimized to transcribe meaning, not sound.

## **3\. On-Device Feasibility for Constrained Edge Hardware**

The mandate that the ORF assessment application must run entirely offline on low-cost Android tablets eliminates the possibility of utilizing cloud APIs or deploying billion-parameter models. The technical architecture must be strictly bounded by the hardware realities of public school deployments.

### **3.1 Establishing the Hardware Baseline**

The DepEd Computerization Program (DCP) issues standardized technical specifications for educational hardware. Based on procurement guidelines for DCP tablets intended for digital learning, the minimum required specifications mandate an Octa-core CPU (typically arranged in a big.LITTLE configuration, such as 4x 1.8 GHz efficiency cores and 4x 2.3 GHz performance cores), usually sourced from budget lines like MediaTek Helio or entry-level Qualcomm Snapdragons.  
Crucially, these tablets are equipped with exactly 4 GB of RAM and 64 GB of internal storage. Because the Android Operating System (typically versions 9.0 through 11.0+) reserves approximately 1.5 to 2.0 GB of memory for system processes and the Android Runtime (ART) environment, the actual contiguous memory available for the ASR application payload is restricted to less than 2.0 GB. This hard ceiling strictly prohibits loading full-precision (FP32 or FP16) neural network weights and necessitates heavy quantization.

### **3.2 Evaluation of Edge Inference Frameworks**

To achieve a Real-Time Factor (RTF) of less than 1.0—meaning the system processes one second of audio in less than one second of compute time—while remaining within the 2 GB memory budget, developers must select highly optimized C++ inference engines with Android Native Development Kit (NDK) bindings.  
**1\.** sherpa-onnx **(Next-Generation Kaldi / K2)**

* **Architecture:** sherpa-onnx provides an incredibly lightweight execution environment for Recurrent Neural Network Transducer (RNN-T) models, such as Zipformer and Conformer architectures.  
* **Footprint and RAM:** It excels in constrained environments. Utilizing INT8 dynamic quantization, a 20-million parameter Zipformer model occupies a mere 46 MB on disk, while a highly accurate 128-layer encoder model occupies roughly 147 MB. In runtime, these models demand only 200 MB to 300 MB of RAM.  
* **Performance (RTF):** On modern ARM CPUs, sherpa-onnx routinely achieves an RTF below 0.1, operating an order of magnitude faster than real-time.  
* **Suitability for ORF:** Extremely High. Unlike Sequence-to-Sequence models, Transducers emit precise, frame-level timestamps for every token and are inherently resistant to text hallucination. Furthermore, they support streaming inference, allowing the app to process the child's voice as they speak rather than waiting for the sentence to conclude.

**2\.** whisper.cpp

* **Architecture:** A zero-dependency, pure C/C++ port of OpenAI’s Whisper model.  
* **Footprint and RAM:** The Whisper Tiny model (39M parameters) occupies about 75 MB, and the Base model (74M parameters) occupies 142 MB. By applying 5-bit or 4-bit GGML quantization (e.g., Q5\_1), memory footprints are minimized, easily operating within a 300 MB to 500 MB RAM envelope.  
* **Performance (RTF):** On budget ARM processors, whisper.cpp typically operates at an RTF of 0.15 to 0.3.  
* **Suitability for ORF:** Low to Moderate. While it satisfies the hardware constraints via efficient JNI bindings, Whisper's underlying architecture is fundamentally flawed for reading assessment. As an autoregressive model, it attempts to predict the next logical word based on context. If a child stutters or misreads a word, Whisper will frequently discard the acoustic evidence and hallucinate the correct word, destroying the integrity of the fluency assessment.

**3\. Moonshine (Useful Sensors)**

* **Architecture:** A specialized encoder-decoder model optimized exclusively for edge devices, circumventing the zero-padding computational waste found in standard transformers.  
* **Footprint and RAM:** Moonshine Tiny possesses a mere 27M parameters. When quantized to 8-bit integers (Q8\_0), the model size is a microscopic 34 MB, with a peak RAM footprint of just 306 MB.  
* **Performance (RTF):** Moonshine sets the benchmark for speed, achieving an RTF of roughly 0.03 to 0.04 on modest hardware. Notably, the Q8\_0 quantization introduces a 0.0% degradation in Word Error Rate compared to the FP16 baseline.  
* **Suitability for ORF:** Moderate. While its extreme efficiency makes it highly attractive for the DepEd tablets, it remains a Sequence-to-Sequence model. Although faster and more accurate than Whisper Tiny, it retains the architectural propensity to auto-correct disfluent speech.

**4\. Vosk**

* **Architecture:** Based on the Kaldi toolkit, Vosk relies on traditional Hidden Markov Models (HMM) paired with Time-Delay Neural Networks (TDNN).  
* **Footprint and RAM:** Vosk's mobile-optimized models are extraordinarily compact, generally around 50 MB, requiring approximately 300 MB of runtime memory.  
* **Suitability for ORF:** Very High. Because Vosk relies on an explicit, external Language Model—typically compiled into a Weighted Finite-State Transducer (WFST) graph—developers exert absolute control over the decoding process. The application can swap the grammar graph on the fly to perfectly match the reading prompt, forcing the engine to evaluate whether the child uttered the specific target words.

The primary engineering decision is not whether the hardware can sustain the compute load, but rather selecting an architecture (Transducer or HMM) that provides robust timestamping and resists language-model hallucination.

## **4\. State-of-the-Art Fine-Tuning Pathways for Low-Resource Child Speech**

Assuming the team successfully implements a mobile inference engine, the next challenge is overcoming the acoustic mismatch of Filipino child speech. Training a model from scratch is mathematically impossible without thousands of hours of data. The state-of-the-art methodology universally relies on fine-tuning pre-trained Self-Supervised Learning (SSL) models.

### **4.1 The Efficacy of the SSL Paradigm**

Models belonging to the wav2vec 2.0 and HuBERT families possess feature extractors (typically convolutional layers) that map raw audio waveforms into latent representations. Because these models are pre-trained on tens of thousands of hours of diverse, unlabeled audio, their latent spaces are incredibly robust to varying acoustic conditions.  
When adapting to child speech, researchers freeze the earliest feature-extraction layers and fine-tune the higher transformer blocks using Connectionist Temporal Classification (CTC) loss against transcribed pediatric data. Empirical studies demonstrate a profound breakthrough regarding data volume requirements: fine-tuning an adult-pretrained wav2vec 2.0 model with **as little as 10 hours of child speech** yields a model that substantially outperforms baseline systems trained on 960 hours of adult speech. This implies that if a development team can secure access to the 8-hour CFSC dataset or a subset of the BK3AT corpus, they possess sufficient data to cross the acoustic threshold required for high-accuracy assessment.

### **4.2 Industry and Academic Precedents**

Several global initiatives have successfully navigated the intersection of low-resource languages and pediatric literacy, providing architectural blueprints for the Philippine context.  
**Wadhwani AI – Vachan Samiksha:** Deployed in public schools across Indian states, Vachan Samiksha is an automated Oral Reading Fluency tool assessing regional languages like Gujarati and Hindi. Because rural classroom audio is highly degraded, their pipeline incorporates a noise normalization layer (utilizing Facebook's denoiser) before feeding the audio into an ensemble of open-source Indian-language ASR models. Rather than relying on the cloud provider's native managed ML services—which resulted in slow auto-scaling and high latency—Wadhwani AI migrated their infrastructure to bare Kubernetes (via TrueFoundry), reducing request latency by 80% and allowing the system to scale horizontally in two minutes rather than nine. Crucially, their assessment module relies on a post-processing alignment algorithm that maps the ASR's character-level output against the target paragraph. This architecture yields a 95% precision rate in recognizing correctly read words (Hits) and an 88% precision rate in identifying missed words (Deletions).  
**Reach Every Reader (Harvard / MIT / FSU):** While Wadhwani AI focuses on broad regional deployment, the Reach Every Reader initiative emphasizes predictive diagnostics. Acknowledging that interventions must occur before a child fails third-grade reading benchmarks, the initiative collected tens of thousands of anonymized kindergarten speech samples. By analyzing pre-literacy speech patterns, researchers demonstrated that early acoustic and linguistic biomarkers are highly predictive of later dyslexia or reading difficulty. To accelerate the development of specialized child ASR, they open-sourced this massive database via data science modeling competitions. Their approach highlights that an effective reading tracker must capture and evaluate the hesitations and disfluencies of early speech, rather than attempting to filter them out.  
**SoapBox Labs and African NLP:** Commercial entities like SoapBox Labs (which focuses explicitly on proprietary pediatric speech engines) and academic consortiums working on African languages (such as the adaptation of wav2vec for Shona or Bati dialects) reiterate a common theme: the integration of acoustic modeling with strict language-specific phonotactics. In low-resource settings, hybrid TDNN-HMM models augmented with data synthesis (pitch manipulation) routinely outperform end-to-end models due to the developer's ability to strictly bind the system's lexicon to the educational curriculum.

## **5\. Algorithmic Approaches to Miscue Detection**

The ultimate objective of the application is not merely transcription, but assessment. A child attempting to read the passage *"Ang aso ay tumakbo"* (The dog ran) might falter, uttering *"Ang a... as... aso ay tu... tumab... tumakbo"*. An end-to-end model like Whisper will utilize its internal language model to "clean up" the transcript, outputting *"Ang aso ay tumakbo"*. The teacher sees a perfect score, while the child's severe decoding struggles are completely masked.  
Automating Oral Reading Fluency requires detecting reading miscues: mispronunciations (substitutions), omitted words (deletions), extra words (insertions), and hesitations or repetitions.

### **5.1 Constrained Decoding and Forced Alignment**

To prevent auto-regressive hallucination, the ASR system must be mathematically constrained by the known reference passage.  
**Graph-Based Decoding (Dynamic LMs):** By utilizing Transducer models (via sherpa-onnx) or Hybrid models (via Vosk), the application can construct a temporary, highly constrained N-gram language model or a Weighted Finite-State Transducer (WFST) graph. The decoding graph is built specifically from the words present in the reading prompt. When the acoustic model emits phonetic probabilities, the decoding algorithm attempts to trace a path through the WFST graph. If the child reads correctly, the path is highly probable. If the child deviates, the decoder searches for phonetically similar alternative paths or explicit error-modeling branches (e.g., mapping common L1-L2 phonetic substitutions). This methodology inherently flags substitutions and deletions without allowing the system to invent logically sound but unuttered words.  
**Forced Alignment Tracking:** Alternatively, the system can utilize forced alignment. In this approach, the ASR engine is provided with both the audio file and the exact text of the reference passage. The algorithm (such as the Kaldi aligner or SCTK) forcibly maps the audio frames to the sequence of phonemes required by the text. Assessment is executed by evaluating the acoustic confidence scores and temporal boundaries of the aligned words. If the confidence score drops below a specific threshold, a mispronunciation or substitution is logged. Furthermore, duration analysis becomes possible; if the temporal boundary of a word stretches significantly longer than the statistical norm, the system flags a "sounding out" hesitation miscue. The CFSC dataset was specifically analyzed to build a two-level Reading Miscue Detector integrating these exact reference verification and word duration metrics.

### **5.2 Scoring Accuracy against Human Raters**

The integration of constrained decoding and Levenshtein (edit) distance calculations allows the system to generate highly accurate Words Correct Per Minute (WCPM) scores. Research utilizing decision-tree classifiers operating on alignment-based features demonstrates that automated miscue detection systems can achieve up to a 91% agreement rate with human expert evaluators. As noted in the Wadhwani AI implementation, precision rates can reach 95% for accurately read words and 88% for deletions, proving that automated assessment is sufficiently rigorous for broad deployment.

## **6\. Strategic Verdict and Risk Assessment**

### **Verdict: Prototype Feasibility for a Two-Person Team**

A two-person student team can absolutely construct a highly functional, offline-first prototype without requiring immediate investment in custom data collection or massive computational resources.  
The strategy relies entirely on an architecture of **Constrained Decoding utilizing pre-trained Edge Models**. The team should bypass training an acoustic model from scratch. Instead, they should deploy a highly optimized framework like sherpa-onnx (using an INT8 quantized Zipformer) or Vosk directly onto the Android tablets. Because these engines execute offline inference at a fraction of real-time, the compute bottleneck is solved.  
To address the lack of a child-specific acoustic model in the initial development phase, the team must implement dynamic language modeling. By tightly constraining the decoding graph to the exact vocabulary of the Grades 1–6 reading passages, the burden on the acoustic model is drastically reduced. The system acts as a phonetic matching engine rather than an open-ended transcriber.  
Once the application architecture—comprising the Android UI, JNI bindings, inference engine, dynamic LM generation, and Levenshtein scoring logic—is validated, the team can negotiate academic access to the CFSC or BK3AT corpora. Utilizing just 10 hours of this specialized data, they can execute a self-supervised fine-tuning pass using CTC loss to adapt the acoustic weights to Filipino child speech, finalizing a production-ready system.

### **The Single Biggest Technical Risk**

The most significant threat to the project's viability is **Architectural Mismatch resulting in Metric Hallucination.**  
There is a profound temptation for developers to select modern, highly popular Sequence-to-Sequence models (such as whisper.cpp or Moonshine) due to their ease of implementation and high generalized accuracy. If the team chooses a Seq2Seq architecture, the project will fundamentally fail its core educational objective. These models rely heavily on powerful internal linguistic priors designed to output grammatically flawless text. When confronted with the stuttering, syllable-by-syllable, disfluent reading of a struggling second grader, a Seq2Seq model will actively ignore the acoustic disruption and "auto-correct" the transcript.  
The resulting Words Correct Per Minute (WCPM) metrics will be artificially inflated, falsely indicating reading proficiency where deep diagnostic intervention is actually required. To mitigate this catastrophic risk, the team must strictly reject unconstrained Seq2Seq models in favor of Transducer or Hybrid architectures that preserve absolute temporal acoustic fidelity and permit rigid, prompt-based decoding constraints.
