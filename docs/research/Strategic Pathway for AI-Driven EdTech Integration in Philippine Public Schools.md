# **Strategic Pathway for AI-Driven EdTech Integration in Philippine Public Schools: Division Pilots, LGU Financing, and Procurement Mechanisms (2026)**

The Philippine basic education sector is navigating an unprecedented era of digital transformation, catalyzed by the integration of artificial intelligence into classroom instruction and shifting national funding paradigms. For emerging education technology (EdTech) enterprises—specifically those developing offline-first AI oral reading assessment applications—navigating the bureaucratic architecture of the Department of Education (DepEd) and Local Government Units (LGUs) requires transitioning from theoretical go-to-market strategies to highly specific, localized execution models.  
This comprehensive report details an actionable roadmap for initiating pilot programs within targeted DepEd Schools Division Offices (SDOs). It provides an exhaustive analysis of identified pilot-friendly divisions, the procedural mechanics of securing a DepEd research permit and Memorandum of Agreement (MOA), the expanded Local Government Unit (LGU) financing pathways under the 2025 Special Education Fund (SEF) guidelines, the restructuring of Official Development Assistance (ODA) following the 2025 dismantling of USAID, and the modernized procurement mechanisms available under the New Government Procurement Act (RA 12009).

## **2\. Procedural Mechanics of Division-Level Pilots in DepEd**

Executing a pilot within a DepEd public school requires strict adherence to bureaucratic protocols. Third-party providers cannot simply deploy software directly to a principal or classroom teacher; they must navigate a structured matrix of partnership guidelines, donation frameworks, and ethical research protocols.

### **The Partnership Framework: DepEd Order 40, s. 2015**

The primary legal vehicle for external engagement is DepEd Order No. 40, s. 2015 ("Guidelines on K to 12 Partnerships"). This directive governs how third parties can implement teacher training, deploy EdTech facilities, and conduct collaborative work within schools. Under this order, any pilot at the individual school level must be formalized via a Memorandum of Agreement (MOA) signed by the School Head. However, if the pilot spans multiple schools across a division, it requires at least a Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) or MOA signed by the Schools Division Superintendent.  
The partnership process mandates a thorough needs analysis and alignment with child protection policies, specifically DepEd Order No. 40, s. 2012 (Child Protection Policy). This is especially critical for an oral reading assessment app, as the software will be utilizing student voice recordings or personal data, thus requiring compliance with stringent child safety and data privacy frameworks.

### **The Adopt-a-School Program (RA 8525\)**

For pre-revenue startups offering their software for free during an initial pilot phase, the Adopt-A-School Program (ASP), established under Republic Act No. 8525, serves as the optimal entry vehicle. Under ASP guidelines, the provision of computer software, technology support, or educational materials is legally classified as a donation.  
Procedurally, the startup must coordinate with the division's School Governance and Operations Division (SGOD), specifically the Social Mobilization and Networking (SocMob) unit, before any deployment. The startup must submit proof of legitimate registration (e.g., SEC or DTI documents) to demonstrate it has been in existence for at least one year and possesses a credible track record. The deployment is subsequently formalized through a Deed of Donation and a Deed of Acceptance, ensuring the software is properly recorded in the school’s inventory systems.

### **Research Permits for Learner-Facing Technologies**

Because an AI oral reading app actively assesses and collects data from elementary learners, it falls under the purview of educational research. Consequently, startups must strictly comply with DepEd Order No. 16, s. 2017 (Research Management Guidelines).  
To conduct a pilot, the startup must submit a formal research proposal to the Schools Division Superintendent. This proposal is evaluated by the Schools Division Research Committee (SDRC), which utilizes a strict Quality Control Checklist to ensure the project aligns with the Basic Education Research Agenda—specifically falling under the "Teaching and Learning" or "Technology & Innovation Management" themes. Ethical clearance is paramount; the startup must provide comprehensive informed consent and assent forms for parents and students, detailing exactly how voice data will be processed, stored, and protected by the offline-first AI. Only after the SDRC issues a formal research permit can the startup commence data gathering and software deployment within the classrooms.

### **Anatomy of a DepEd Pilot MOA and Timelines**

A standard pilot MOA for EdTech deployment typically takes 3 to 6 months to negotiate and execute, encompassing the initial proposal, SDRC ethical review, and legal review by the SGOD. The MOA generally contains the following crucial clauses:

1. **Scope and Duration:** Explicitly defining the number of pilot schools, target grade levels, and the exact timeline of the intervention (e.g., an 8-week reading remediation cycle).  
2. **Roles and Responsibilities:** DepEd agrees to provide access to learners and ensure teacher participation, while the startup agrees to provide the software, technical support, and onboarding training.  
3. **Data Privacy and Child Protection:** Strict adherence to the Data Privacy Act of 2012 and DO 40, s. 2012\. The startup must guarantee that student voice recordings are anonymized, stored securely (or processed entirely offline), and not monetized.  
4. **Intellectual Property:** Acknowledgment that the core algorithms and software remain the proprietary intellectual property of the startup, while aggregate, anonymized reading data may be shared with DepEd for policy formulation.

### **Startup Precedents in DepEd**

Documented examples demonstrate that local startups can successfully navigate this bureaucracy. Frontlearners, an interactive digital learning provider, secured a massive pilot across 510 schools nationwide (30 schools per region). They achieved this by aligning their deployment with DepEd's evaluation of technology-enabled instruction and offline capability, specifically targeting Last Mile Schools where internet connectivity is absent but local area networks (LAN) or solar-powered devices can operate. Similarly, Adarna House secured a localized pilot for its "Handang Magbasa" and "WIKAHON" reading programs in Quezon City. They bypassed the slower national procurement channels by partnering directly with the municipal Education Affairs Unit, which funded and facilitated the intervention for 300 Key Stage 1 teachers.

## **3\. The LGU Pathway and the Special Education Fund (SEF)**

While DepEd oversees pedagogical approval and curriculum standards, local government units (LGUs) hold substantial financial leverage over public school infrastructure and technology through the Special Education Fund (SEF). For an EdTech startup seeking eventual paid procurement, the LGU pathway is often faster, more agile, and better capitalized than national DepEd channels.

### **The Expansion of the SEF (Joint Circular No. 1, s. 2025\)**

The SEF is generated from a 1% levy on the assessed value of real property within a municipality or city, providing a supplementary budget for local schools. Historically, the use of the SEF was heavily restricted, primarily allocated for physical infrastructure, utility bills, and sports activities. This rigid framework made the procurement of software or digital subscriptions highly risky for local auditors.  
This paradigm shifted dramatically with the issuance of Joint Circular No. 1, s. 2025 by DepEd, the Department of Budget and Management (DBM), the Department of the Interior and Local Government (DILG), and the Department of Finance (DOF). This landmark policy officially broadened the allowable expenses of the SEF to align with modern educational crises. Crucially for EdTech startups, the SEF can now legally fund the Academic Recovery and Accessible Learning (ARAL) Program (RA 12028), which explicitly covers targeted remedial instruction, literacy remediation, and academic recovery interventions. Furthermore, the circular institutionalized evidence-based budgeting, mandating that the Local School Board (LSB)—which manages the SEF—prioritize interventions that directly improve foundational literacy and numeracy based on actual learning outcomes.

### **Quezon City's SEF Utilization and Budget Priorities**

Quezon City possesses one of the largest SEF budgets in the country, managed by the Quezon City School Board, which is co-chaired by Mayor Joy Belmonte and SDO Superintendent Dr. Carleen Sedilla. A review of recent SEF appropriations reveals a strong willingness to fund digital and reading interventions. In 2023, the city’s SEF budget reached Php 2.36 billion. Within this budget, the city routinely allocates hundreds of millions for "Internet Expenses" (Php 105 million) and "Textbooks and Instructional Materials" (Php 247 million). Furthermore, it specifically categorizes millions for "Computer Software" (Php 2.98 million) and "Information and Communication Tech Eqpt" (Php 97.9 million). The presence of the Education Affairs Unit within the QC LGU further facilitates the rapid deployment of these funds into schools, acting as a bridge between the Mayor's office and SDO QC.

### **Leveraging the Startup QC Ecosystem**

For a student startup based in Quezon City, the Startup QC program acts as a powerful catalyst for LGU procurement and credibility. Established under Ordinance SP-3109, S-2022 to position the city as the innovation capital of the Philippines, the program offers equity-free grants of up to Php 1,000,000 for professional startups and up to Php 100,000 for student innovators participating in the business plan competition.  
The program has a proven track record of nurturing EdTech and socially impactful enterprises. Alumni include Kippap Education, which utilizes gamified learning and video lectures to boost licensure exam pass rates, and Agapai, an AI-powered developmental screening tool for parents. Furthermore, the LGU actively adopts the technologies produced by its incubatees. For example, PlastiPave, a sustainable paving block developed by Ateneo de Manila University students who won the Startup QC competition, was subsequently pilot-tested in various sites directly by the Quezon City government.  
The Local Economic Development and Investment Promotions Office (LEDIPO), which manages Startup QC, actively bridges startups with industries and government agencies. By securing recognition within Startup QC, an EdTech venture effectively gains a "green lane" to pilot discussions. The LGU endorsement validates the technology's credibility, mitigating operational risk for LSB procurement officers and SDO superintendents.

## **4\. Current State of Official Development Assistance (ODA) in 2026**

For decades, the United States Agency for International Development (USAID) was the primary architect and funder of early-grade reading interventions in the Philippines. However, a seismic geopolitical shift in 2025 radically altered the ODA landscape, requiring EdTech startups to pivot their institutional partnership strategies away from traditional American funding.

### **The Dismantling of USAID**

In January 2025, the incoming Trump administration issued an executive order placing a near-total freeze on foreign development assistance. By March 2025, it was announced that 83% of USAID's programs and approximately 5,200 contracts would be permanently canceled. On July 1, 2025, USAID officially ceased operations as an independent agency, with its minimal remaining functions absorbed by the U.S. State Department.  
This dismantling severely impacted the Philippine education sector. The flagship ABC+ (Advancing Basic Education in the Philippines) project—implemented by RTI International alongside DepEd and The Asia Foundation—was heavily funded by USAID to improve early literacy, numeracy, and socio-emotional skills in Regions V, VI, and BARMM. The sudden withdrawal of this financial backbone abruptly halted the procurement of supplementary reading materials, the deployment of decodable books in Mother Tongue languages, and the localized teacher training initiatives that the program supported.

### **Alternative Funding Pathways in 2026**

With traditional US-backed development aid eviscerated, the funding void is rapidly being filled by multilateral organizations, regional partnerships, and highly localized philanthropic efforts.

* **UNICEF and Bilateral Partners:** In January 2026, UNICEF, in collaboration with the Government of Finland, launched a massive new Digital Education Strategy. The Philippines was identified as one of the 18 priority countries for the initial rollout. This strategy explicitly focuses on scaling innovative, AI-enabled teaching and learning programs to combat the global learning crisis in reading and math.  
* **ASEAN Foundation & Google.org:** As evidenced by the January 2026 launch of Project AGAP.AI, regional coalitions and private tech philanthropy are aggressively funding AI literacy and capacity-building programs across Philippine public schools. This initiative targets the integration of AI tools for 1.05 million learners and 300,000 teachers.  
* **Asian Development Bank (ADB):** The ADB continues to finance EdTech hardware and software, particularly focusing on offline and blended learning for geographically isolated areas. Their interventions support "Last Mile Schools" by deploying low-cost tablets connected to local area networks (LAN) supported by solar-powered battery antennas.  
* **Local Philanthropy (Synergeia, Ayala, Aboitiz):** Domestic non-governmental organizations like the Synergeia Foundation exert immense influence over LGU education policies. Working directly with Local School Boards, Synergeia has orchestrated highly successful reading remediation programs. For example, their Proof of Concept model in Iloilo raised Grade 3 reading proficiency from 30% to 90% in one year through localized teacher training and progress tracking. Aligning an AI reading assessment tool with Synergeia's data-driven LSB frameworks presents a highly viable B2B (business-to-NGO) distribution channel. Similarly, the Gokongwei Brothers Foundation actively supports digital pilots, recently aiding the rollout of Khan Academy's platform in 34 schools.

## **5\. Navigating Post-Pilot Procurement (RA 12009\)**

Assuming a successful pilot, transitioning from a free deployment to a paid contract requires navigating public procurement laws. In August 2024, the Philippine government enacted Republic Act No. 12009, the New Government Procurement Act (NGPA), with its Implementing Rules and Regulations (IRR) taking effect in February 2025\. The NGPA replaced the archaic RA 9184, introducing modernized, fit-for-purpose modalities that greatly benefit software-as-a-service (SaaS) and technology startups. Under RA 12009, "computer software and products of the creative industry" are explicitly classified as intangible goods, streamlining their acquisition.

### **Small Value Procurement (SVP)**

SVP remains a primary mechanism for procuring software licenses that do not warrant full competitive bidding. Under RA 12009, the SVP threshold for national agencies and highly urbanized LGUs has been standardized to Php 2,000,000. A critical reform in the new law allows Procuring Entities to send Requests for Quotations (RFQs) to at least three suppliers; however, if only *one* valid quotation is received, the agency is now legally permitted to proceed with the evaluation and award the contract. This eliminates the previous bureaucratic paralysis caused by failed canvassing, significantly accelerating the sales cycle for niche EdTech solutions.

### **Direct Acquisition**

For minor subscriptions or initial departmental rollouts, RA 12009 introduced the Direct Acquisition mode. This applies to non-Common-Use Supplies and Equipment (non-CSE) with an Approved Budget for the Contract (ABC) not exceeding Php 200,000. Under Direct Acquisition, an SDO or LGU can purchase software directly from a known, reputable source without the need for canvassing, multiple quotations, or prior approval from the Bids and Awards Committee (BAC), provided the item is listed in the Annual Procurement Plan (APP). This is the ideal vehicle for landing an initial paid contract at the school or district level, allowing an EdTech startup to convert pilot users into paying customers rapidly.

### **Direct Procurement for Science, Technology, and Innovation (STI)**

Perhaps the most revolutionary addition for a student startup is Section 37 of RA 12009: Direct Procurement for Science, Technology, and Innovation. This modality allows a Procuring Entity to bypass competitive bidding entirely and procure goods and services directly from a "qualified startup business".  
This mode covers supplies, equipment, and related services used exclusively for research and development (R\&D), as well as products resulting from a commissioned task by the government. If a division commissions the startup to customize the offline AI app for their specific localized Mother Tongue, they can use this modality. Crucially, the IRR stipulates that unless otherwise indicated in a written agreement, the intellectual property (IP) rights belong to the Procuring Entity. Startups must strictly negotiate IP clauses during contracting to ensure they retain ownership of their underlying AI algorithms while granting the government a perpetual license to the customized output.
