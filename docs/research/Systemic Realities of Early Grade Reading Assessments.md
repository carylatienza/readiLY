# **Systemic Realities of Early Grade Reading Assessments in the Philippine Basic Education Sector: A Comprehensive Landscape Analysis**

The Philippine basic education system is currently navigating a profound and widely documented literacy crisis. This vulnerability has been exacerbated by pandemic-induced learning losses and long-standing systemic resource constraints. Recent international and national assessments, including the Programme for International Student Assessment (PISA) and reports from the Second Congressional Commission on Education (EDCOM II), highlight that a substantial proportion of Filipino learners reach the intermediate grades lacking fundamental decoding and reading comprehension skills. In response to this national emergency, the Department of Education (DepEd) mandates two primary diagnostic reading assessments: the Philippine Informal Reading Inventory (Phil-IRI) and the Comprehensive Rapid Literacy Assessment (CRLA).  
This report provides an exhaustive, evidence-based synthesis of the actual day-to-day administration of these assessments, capturing the structural, technological, and psychosocial realities of public elementary school teachers handling Grades 1 through 6\. By juxtaposing official policy with ground-level practice, assessing technological readiness, evaluating artificial intelligence adoption pilots, and mapping the compliance value chain, this analysis delineates the operational landscape into which any automated oral reading assessment application must seamlessly integrate.

## **The Assessment Implementation Gap: Official Mandates Versus Classroom Realities**

There exists a significant dichotomy between the standardized pedagogical protocols envisioned by DepEd's central administration and the survival-driven realities of the public school classroom. The administration of both the Phil-IRI and the CRLA is heavily affected by extreme student-to-teacher ratios, compounding administrative burdens, and highly constrained instructional time.

### **The Official Assessment Framework**

According to DepEd guidelines, the reading assessment ecosystem operates on a highly structured dual-tool framework designed to track reading progress across key stages of basic education.  
The Comprehensive Rapid Literacy Assessment (CRLA) was initially conceived in response to the 32-week educational gap caused by the COVID-19 pandemic to help teachers rapidly evaluate returning learners. Designed for Key Stage 1 (Grades 1 to 3), the CRLA is intended to be a low-tech, multimodal, teacher-led diagnostic tool. The official administration time is estimated at eight to fifteen minutes per student, per language. The languages assessed scale by grade level: Grade 1 focuses primarily on the Mother Tongue; Grade 2 assesses both Mother Tongue and Filipino; and Grade 3 encompasses Mother Tongue, Filipino, and English. The CRLA utilizes a tiered task system, beginning with phonological awareness, alphabet knowledge, and decoding, before progressing to reading fluency and comprehension. Based on the resulting scores, learners are categorized into specific reading profiles that dictate their subsequent intervention needs.

| CRLA Reading Profile | Official Description and Intervention Requirement |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Full Refresher** | The learner possesses minimal phonological awareness or letter recognition. Requires direct instruction of concepts and foundational skills from the previous grade level. |
| **Moderate Refresher** | The learner recognizes some letters and rhymes but lacks a firm grasp of prior grade-level concepts. Requires significant assistance and guided practice. |
| **Light Refresher** | The learner has a firm grasp of previous concepts and decodes simple words but requires practice to improve automaticity and accuracy. |
| **Grade Ready** | The learner's reading ability aligns with previous end-of-year standards. The learner comprehends grade-level texts with automaticity and accuracy. |

Conversely, the Philippine Informal Reading Inventory (Phil-IRI) targets Key Stage 2 and beyond, specifically Grades 3 to 6 for Filipino and Grades 4 to 6 for English. The Phil-IRI is a much more exhaustive assessment operating in four distinct stages. Stage 1 involves the Group Screening Test (GST), a 20- to 40-item multiple-choice silent reading comprehension test administered to the entire class within a 30- to 60-minute timeframe. Learners who score below a specific threshold (typically 14 out of 20\) are flagged for Stage 2, which requires the administration of Individualized Graded Passages. During Stage 2, the teacher listens to the student read aloud, manually recording reading miscues—such as mispronunciations, omissions, substitutions, and insertions—while timing the student to calculate words-per-minute fluency. The teacher then asks oral comprehension questions. The results classify the learner's proficiency on three levels: Independent, Instructional, or Frustration. Following specialized intervention (Stage 3), a Post-test (Stage 4\) is administered to measure progress.

### **Ground-Level Execution, Time Constraints, and Methodological Workarounds**

In practice, the meticulous individualized testing mandated by these frameworks frequently collapses under logistical weight. Action research, thesis reports, and teacher testimonies consistently cite time constraints as the primary barrier to assessment fidelity.  
The mathematical reality of the classroom renders strict compliance nearly impossible. A teacher handling a class of forty to fifty students is expected to dedicate eight to fifteen minutes per student for the CRLA. Assessing a single language requires five to twelve hours of total instructional time. For Grade 3 teachers assessing three languages, completing the CRLA demands a massive diversion of hours away from regular curriculum delivery. Similarly, the Phil-IRI Stage 2 oral reading assessment requires the teacher to conduct one-on-one evaluations for every student who failed the GST, an undertaking that can paralyze a classroom if a large majority of the class falls into the Frustration category, as is often the case in post-pandemic cohorts.  
Consequently, the workload forces educators into necessary systemic compromises and workarounds. Because the Phil-IRI requires a highly complex recording of reading miscues while simultaneously operating a stopwatch and administering comprehension questions, the cognitive load on the teacher is severe. To circumvent this bottleneck, educators engage in what is colloquially known in the system as "window dressing" or compliance-driven administration. Individualized oral testing is frequently abbreviated, rushed, or skipped entirely. Instead of strict tool adherence, some teachers estimate the oral fluency of students based on their daily classroom observations or conduct rapid, informal group assessments.  
The data encoding process introduces another layer of friction. Teachers must manually transfer results from paper learner sheets to automated Excel scoring templates provided by the Bureau of Learning Delivery. This manual transfer is highly prone to clerical error. Driven by the pressure of Division Office deadlines, there is widespread reliance on rapid data replication, where teachers copy and paste scores across forms simply to satisfy administrative requirements, thereby compromising the diagnostic validity of the data. Furthermore, because teachers are overwhelmed with paperwork, the highly individualized reading interventions theoretically required for learners at the Frustration or Full Refresher levels are rarely implemented with fidelity; instead, schools default to generalized, whole-class remedial approaches.

## **The Burden of Compliance: Educator Pain Points and Perverse Incentives**

A deeper analysis of documented teacher grievances—sourced from qualitative academic studies, public forums, and education advocacy networks—reveals severe institutional pressures that dictate how reading assessments are handled and reported.

### **The Dynamics of the "No Read, No Pass" Policy**

A central locus of frustration for elementary educators is the tension between the mandate to eradicate illiteracy and the administrative pressure to advance students to the next grade level. While local policies emphasize "No Read, No Pass" or "Zero Non-Readers in Grade 4" to ensure foundational literacy, the systemic reality strongly discourages retaining students.  
Testimonies from educators highlight a deep cynicism regarding the bureaucratic definition of a "reader." Teachers report instances where students who are essentially "non-comprehenders"—meaning they can mechanically decode sounds or words but fail to answer basic comprehension questions—are institutionally reclassified as readers to improve the school's literacy metrics. Educators detail how they spend an entire school year warning parents and students about potential retention due to illiteracy, only to be instructed by school heads to pass the students anyway, forcing teachers to inflate failing grades to passing marks to pull the students up.  
The pressure from the educational hierarchy creates a powerful disincentive for honest reporting. Teachers who accurately report that a significant majority of their class falls into the Frustration reading level invite intense administrative scrutiny. They face being summoned by District Supervisors to explain high failure rates, endure implications that they are ineffective educators, and are burdened with submitting exhaustive evidence of intervention logs and home visitations. Consequently, there is an unspoken institutional incentive to manipulate reading assessment data. Lowering the reported number of struggling readers mathematically reduces the teacher's required intervention paperwork, creating a system where data is engineered to fit acceptable administrative narratives rather than reflecting true student capabilities.

### **Bureaucratic Overload and Collateral Damage**

Educators frequently describe themselves as collateral damage within a highly bureaucratic and politically driven educational system. Teachers explicitly state that they are bombarded with teaching tasks and overwhelmed by the volume of required paperwork. The reading assessment process alone requires the management of numerous documents: Form 1A/B for screening results, Form 3A/B for class reading profiles, and Form 4A/B for consolidated school profiles.  
These reading-specific forms are in addition to the standard required documentation, which includes the Learner Information System (LIS) encoding, the standard School Forms (SF1 through SF10), Individual Learning Monitoring Plans, and the newly mandated Academic Recovery and Accessible Learning (ARAL) program tagging. Therefore, any proposed technological solution that automates oral assessment but introduces yet another isolated dashboard to monitor will likely be viewed by the teaching force as an exacerbation of their administrative burden rather than a genuine pedagogical relief.

## **Technological Readiness: The Infrastructure and Hardware Chasm**

The successful deployment of an automated oral reading application is entirely predicated on the availability of functional hardware and reliable internet connectivity. A thorough review of the technological reality in Philippine public schools reveals a highly fragmented and frequently deficient infrastructure.

### **The DepEd Computerization Program (DCP) Realities**

To bridge the digital divide, the government implements the DepEd Computerization Program (DCP), which aims to distribute e-classroom packages consisting of laptops, desktops, tablet PCs, and smart televisions to public schools nationwide. Prior to recent budgetary cycles, DepEd successfully distributed over 1.25 million devices to approximately 93 percent of public schools. However, when measured against the massive student population of over 24 million learners, the computer-to-student ratio is critically low; if devices were assigned individually to students, only 6 percent of the enrollment could be accommodated. As a result, DCP devices are predominantly utilized by teachers rather than students.  
Furthermore, the mere presence of a DCP package does not guarantee sustained operational utility. Academic evaluations of the DCP reveal several critical failure points in the hardware lifecycle:

* **Strict Warranties and Maintenance Bottlenecks:** The deployment of DCP equipment is governed by stringent supplier policies. Cases of voided warranties include unauthorized opening of the item, water damage, electrical damage from external power sources, and unauthorized repair using third-party service centers. Because rural schools are not permitted to repair equipment independently, damaged hardware frequently sits in prolonged disuse while awaiting official supplier intervention.  
* **Physical Infrastructure Deficiencies:** Many schools struggle with fundamental facility issues that endanger sensitive electronics. Broken electrical outlets, uncertified electrical wirings, inadequate ventilation, and a lack of secure storage spaces with window grills expose the equipment to environmental degradation and theft, severely limiting the program's effectiveness.  
* **The Smartphone Paradigm:** While personal smartphone ownership among teachers is exceptionally high—with educators routinely utilizing their own data and devices to augment school deficiencies—student access to capable smartphones is heavily restricted by socio-economic constraints. An app requiring student-owned hardware is structurally unfeasible in the public basic education sector.

### **Connectivity and Electricity Constraints**

Official data indicates that the Department of Education has connected approximately 64 percent of public schools to the internet and has distributed millions of SIM cards with data allocations to the teaching force. However, "connected" is a relative term; in many schools, internet access implies a single, low-bandwidth connection restricted to the principal's office or the ICT laboratory, rather than ubiquitous, high-speed Wi-Fi covering all classrooms.  
For the thousands of "Last Mile Schools" situated in geographically isolated and disadvantaged areas (GIDA), the situation is even more precarious. These schools frequently experience intermittent electricity and must rely on newly procured, varying-quality satellite internet connections. Therefore, an automated reading assessment application that requires a persistent, high-bandwidth connection to process voice data in real-time via the cloud will fail catastrophically in the majority of Philippine classrooms. Offline capability—allowing local audio capture and processing, with subsequent asynchronous cloud synchronization—is an absolute prerequisite for national viability.

## **Artificial Intelligence in the Classroom: Acceptance, Technostress, and the Microsoft Pilots**

The integration of artificial intelligence (AI) and automated voice recognition into reading assessments is not merely theoretical in the Philippines; it is an ongoing reality that yields vital lessons regarding technological limitations, pedagogical efficacy, and educator psychology.

### **The Microsoft Reading Progress Initiatives**

Since late 2021, DepEd has partnered extensively with Microsoft to pilot "Reading Progress," an AI-powered fluency tracking tool integrated into the Microsoft Teams environment. This tool automates the recording of reading accuracy, speed, and pronunciation, and has been piloted in diverse divisions including Bais City, Dumaguete, Cabanatuan, and Quezon City.  
The empirical results reported from these pilots are highly compelling:

* **Administrative Efficiency:** In Bais City, educators reported a transformative reduction in assessment time. What previously required sixteen hours (two full days) of manual, one-on-one evaluation to assess a single class was reduced to just two hours using the automated tool, drastically freeing up time for lesson planning and targeted intervention.  
* **Literacy Advancement:** In Cabanatuan City's READExcel pilot program, focused interventions utilizing the AI tool resulted in 100 percent of targeted learners in three school districts advancing to higher literacy levels. Pre-test data showing substantial populations of struggling readers dropped to zero following consistent, technology-supported practice.

Despite these high-level successes, ground-level application reveals acute pedagogical and technical hurdles. Early-grade learners (Grades 1 to 3\) are generally unaccustomed to reading into a microphone, which often induces shyness, performance anxiety, and a fear of making mistakes in front of a device, artificially depressing their fluency scores. Additionally, the physical acoustic environment of a Philippine public school—typically characterized by open-air classrooms without soundproofing, the ambient noise of neighboring sections, and a lack of high-quality noise-canceling headsets—interferes significantly with the accuracy of automated voice recognition algorithms. Furthermore, the AI must contend with identifying proper pronunciation amidst a vast array of heavy regional dialects, indigenous accents, and the unique phonological transitions of Filipino children reading in a second or third language.

### **Teacher Resistance and the Technostress Phenomenon**

While many educators acknowledge the administrative relief provided by automated tools, the Filipino teaching force exhibits significant levels of "technostress." Technostress is defined as the physical and psychological strain experienced when attempting to adapt to new digital technologies, characterized by feelings of techno-invasion, techno-overload, and techno-complexity. The abrupt, forced digitalization during the COVID-19 pandemic left many teachers suffering from cognitive strain, blurred work-life boundaries, and emotional fatigue.  
Research analyzing technology acceptance among Filipino teachers reveals a complex landscape:

* **The Competency Gap and Tool Fatigue:** While pre-service and younger teachers demonstrate higher digital literacy, many incumbent educators in rural areas report severe difficulties navigating digital spreadsheets, managing login credentials, and troubleshooting software glitches. Teachers explicitly cite the steep learning curve of new digital tools as a primary barrier to implementation. A digital tool that adds complex workflows will face quiet, passive abandonment.  
* **Role Conflict and Job Insecurity:** Although 65 percent of surveyed teachers are familiar with AI in education, there is an underlying anxiety regarding the potential for AI to diminish the personal teacher-student connection. Teachers worry that over-reliance on algorithms threatens the empathy and rapport necessary for early childhood education, alongside lingering, albeit unfounded, fears of eventual job displacement.

## **The Bureaucratic Compliance Chain: Reporting Workflows and Data Privacy**

To successfully deploy an automated oral reading application, the software's architecture must mirror and respect DepEd's rigid reporting value chain. A mobile application that functions beautifully but generates proprietary reports is functionally useless to a teacher who must encode specific data points into mandated government systems.

### **Assessment Cadence and System Coexistence**

Reading assessments in the Philippines are strictly governed by standardized temporal windows aimed at tracking longitudinal progress. The Beginning of School Year (BoSY) assessments are conducted between July and September to establish a diagnostic baseline. Subsequently, the Middle of School Year (MoSY) and End of School Year (EoSY) assessments are administered between February and May to evaluate the efficacy of the interventions and determine the final reading profiles.  
The definitive source of truth for all educational data in the Philippines is the Learner Information System (LIS) and the Basic Education Information System (BEIS). Teachers utilize the LIS to update enrollment statuses, track transfers, and apply specialized programmatic tags, including the newly mandated ARAL (Academic Recovery and Accessible Learning) tagging intended for struggling learners.  
Currently, results for the CRLA and Phil-IRI are consolidated by the teacher into automated Excel spreadsheets. These class-level results are subsequently rolled up to the School Head, transmitted to the Division Office, and eventually uploaded to the national Bureau of Learning Delivery (BLD) Numeracy and Literacy Assessments Submission Portal. Any new technological application must, at an absolute minimum, possess the capability to export assessment data in a CSV or Excel format that precisely replicates the columns, naming conventions, and structural hierarchy of the official DepEd CRLA and Phil-IRI templates.

### **Data Privacy, Parental Consent, and Ethical Compliance**

Developing an application that records the voices of minors and processes their educational proficiency data invokes strict regulatory compliance under the Philippine Data Privacy Act of 2012 (Republic Act 10173). The National Privacy Commission (NPC) mandates stringent protections for children, treating learners under the age of 13 as a highly vulnerable class requiring the highest tier of data protection.

* **Verifiable Parental Consent:** The lawful processing of audio recordings of minors for an automated assessment requires explicit, verifiable, and documented parental consent. DepEd mandates strict Data Privacy Consent forms for any activity that falls outside standard curriculum delivery. Gathering, securing, and archiving physical consent forms for every learner at the beginning of the school year presents a massive logistical hurdle for teachers.  
* **Data Residency and Vendor Accountability:** The NPC requires that educational technologies generating high-risk data (such as AI-driven proficiency profiling) conduct a Privacy Impact Assessment (PIA) and ensure data minimization. Cloud-based processing of student voices presents significant legal complications if the servers are located offshore, or if the audio data is subsequently utilized to train third-party commercial AI models without explicit disclosure. Furthermore, educational institutions and third-party developers are strictly prohibited from utilizing student data for behavioral advertising or public profiling.

## **Decentralized Governance: Tool Adoption and Pilot Approvals**

Navigating the procurement and official adoption of a new educational tool within DepEd requires an understanding of its decentralized governance model, heavily anchored in the principles of School-Based Management (SBM).

| Level of Governance | Key Decision Maker | Authority and Role in Tool Adoption |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **School Level** | **School Head / Principal** | Empowered under SBM (RA 9155\) to manage the school's Maintenance and Other Operating Expenses (MOOE) fund. The principal has the autonomy to approve localized pilots, contextualized reading interventions, and the procurement of supplementary digital tools, provided they align with the School Improvement Plan (SIP). |
| **District / Division Level** | **Schools Division Superintendent (SDS) & Education Program Supervisor (EPS)** | The EPS for English or Filipino monitors literacy program compliance and provides technical assistance across the division. The SDS holds the authority to approve division-wide rollouts, allocate Human Resource Training and Development (HRTD) funds for large-scale teacher training, and validate consolidated assessment data. |
| **Regional / Central Level** | **Bureau of Learning Delivery (BLD) & ICT Service** | National policy directives, such as the official endorsement of the Microsoft Reading Progress tool, are forged at the Central Office level. A mobile application aspiring for nationwide official status must be rigorously vetted by the BLD for pedagogical alignment and the ICT Service for data security and infrastructure compatibility. |

For a technology developer seeking to pilot a new automated oral reading application, the most frictionless point of entry is the School Head. Under the SBM framework, Principals possess the legal autonomy to adopt supplementary technological tools for localized reading interventions—such as incorporating an app during "Catch-Up Fridays" or dedicated remedial reading periods—without requiring complex approval from the Central Office. This localized adoption is viable provided the tool is free or funded via MOOE/Parent-Teacher Association (PTA) contributions, and crucially, does not replace the mandatory official submission of standardized DepEd forms to the Division Office.

## **Synthesized Conclusions and Strategic Blind Spots**

### **(a) Evidence Contradicting the Assumption that Teachers Universally Desire Automation**

While automated assessments theoretically promise vast administrative efficiency, substantial evidence suggests that teachers harbor deep pedagogical and psychological reservations about removing the human element from oral reading evaluations.

1. **Pedagogical Loss of Diagnostic Nuance:** Reading assessments are fundamentally diagnostic, not merely evaluative. When a child struggles to decode a word, a teacher intuitively observes holistic behavioral cues—such as squinting indicative of vision issues, physical manifestations of anxiety, or highly specific speech impediments. An automated tool grades the audio output but remains entirely blind to the behavioral context, a context that teachers rely on heavily to build empathy, design specific interventions, and foster rapport.  
2. **Exacerbation of Test Anxiety:** Data from the Microsoft Reading Progress pilots indicate that young, vulnerable learners (Grades 1 to 3\) are frequently intimidated by the act of reading into a digital device. The absence of a reassuring, supportive human presence can artificially depress their reading fluency scores, leading to inaccurate profiling and inappropriate placements.  
3. **Technostress and Systemic Friction:** Teachers explicitly report that the process of integrating new digital technologies—which includes troubleshooting software bugs, managing learner login credentials, handling device malfunctions, and waiting for files to upload on slow networks—frequently consumes more time than conducting a rapid manual assessment. The perception of learning "yet another system" immediately triggers resistance.  
4. **Skepticism Toward AI Accuracy:** Educators remain highly skeptical of an AI's ability to accurately and fairly evaluate learners who speak with heavy regional dialects, indigenous languages, or the unique phonological characteristics of Filipino children transitioning from Mother Tongue to English instruction.  
5. **The Threat of Transparent Data:** Because the current system places immense pressure on teachers to eliminate "Frustration" level readers, educators have grown accustomed to manipulating manual assessment data to survive administrative scrutiny. An AI tool that objectively and immutably records a disastrously high failure rate removes the teacher's ability to "fudge" the numbers, potentially exposing the school to punitive measures from the Division Office.

### **(b) Ten Critical Open Questions for Primary Educator Interviews**

The existing academic literature and official policy documents map the macro-level systemic landscape but leave crucial operational micro-interactions unaddressed. To engineer a truly user-centric application, the following specific questions must be directed to teachers during primary research interviews:

1. **Acoustic Management:** *"Given the severe ambient noise of a typical open-air, multi-section public school classroom, how and where do you physically isolate a student to conduct an oral reading assessment without disruption?"*  
2. **Device Sharing and Liability:** *"If an assessment application requires a smartphone or tablet to record the student, will you be forced to use your personal device? How comfortable are you handing your personal, unsecured smartphone to forty different children over the course of a day?"*  
3. **Offline Data Workflows:** *"If the application functions entirely offline to bypass poor school internet, how often and through what specific means (e.g., taking the device home to use personal Wi-Fi) would you realistically be able to sync the assessment data to the cloud for report generation?"*  
4. **Data Portability and Integration:** *"Walk me through the exact, step-by-step process of transferring a student's oral reading score from your notes into the LIS or the automated Excel sheet. What specific file format must the app's data export utilize to completely eliminate your manual re-typing?"*  
5. **Micro-Intervention Dynamics:** *"When a student makes a critical miscue—such as a mispronunciation or omission—during a manual Phil-IRI test, do you correct them instantly, or wait until the passage is finished? Should the automated app provide real-time audio corrections to the student, or remain completely silent?"*  
6. **Dialect and Accent Tolerance:** *"What are the most common regional pronunciation quirks or dialect-driven 'miscues' your students make that you personally accept as correct, but that a rigid, native-English-trained AI might unfairly penalize?"*  
7. **Consent Form Logistics:** *"To legally record and process a student's voice, we must obtain verifiable parental consent. What is the most realistic and frictionless way for you to distribute, collect, and archive forty physical consent forms from parents at the beginning of the school year?"*  
8. **The Reality of Workarounds:** *"Off the record, if you are severely pressed for time during the BoSY window, how do you estimate a student's reading level without conducting the full fifteen-minute, one-on-one CRLA assessment? How could an application legitimize and accelerate this necessary 'shortcut'?"*  
9. **Navigating the "Zero Non-Reader" Pressure:** *"Does the intense pressure from the Division Office to lower the number of 'Frustration' level readers affect how strictly you grade oral reading? If the app objectively and unalterably reports a high failure rate for your class, would you view the technology as a liability rather than a help?"*  
10. **School-Level Adoption Authority:** *"If I offer this application to your classroom for free, who exactly holds the final, definitive authority to authorize its use during your instructional time? Must you seek formal approval from the Principal, the District Supervisor, or the Division Reading Coordinator?"*
